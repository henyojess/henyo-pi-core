# Source Credibility Guide

A structured framework for evaluating source quality during deep research.

## Credibility Dimensions

Evaluate each source across these dimensions:

### 1. Authorship
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| High | Named author with verifiable credentials |
| Medium | Organization with reputation, but anonymous author |
| Low | Anonymous, unattributed, or unclear authorship |

### 2. Publication Venue
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| High | Peer-reviewed journal, major news org, official agency |
| Medium | Reputable blog, industry publication, conference |
| Low | Unknown website, personal blog, social media |

### 3. Citation Network
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| High | Multiple independent sources confirm; paper cites well |
| Medium | One or two corroborating sources exist |
| Low | Only source is self-citation or no other source agrees |

### 4. Date Currency
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| Current | Published within 1 year (fast-moving fields: within 3 months) |
| Reasonable | Published within 2-3 years (mature fields: 5+ years) |
| Stale | Published >5 years (may be obsolete for this topic) |

### 5. Transparency
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| High | Clear methodology, data sources, funding disclosed |
| Medium | Some data sources mentioned, gaps in methodology |
| Low | No methodology, no data sources, promotional language |

### 6. Cross-Verification
| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| High | 3+ independent sources agree on facts |
| Medium | 2 sources agree, or one strong source with one supporting |
| Low | Single source only, or conflicting information |

## Domain Trust Matrix

| Domain Category | Typical Trust | Examples |
|----------------|---------------|----------|
| Academic Journals | High | Nature, Science, arXiv (check peer-review status) |
| Government (.gov) | High | NASA, NOAA, CDC, Bureau of Labor Statistics |
| International Organizations | High | UN, World Bank, IMF, WHO |
| Major News Outlets | High | Reuters, AP, Bloomberg, Financial Times |
| Tech Publications | Medium-High | IEEE Spectrum, ACM, TechCrunch (check bias) |
| Industry Reports | Medium | Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey (check sponsor) |
| Company Materials | Medium | 10-K filings (high), press releases (promotional) |
| Social Media | Low-Medium | Twitter/X threads, LinkedIn posts (verify claims) |
| Forums/Communities | Low | Reddit, Stack Overflow, Hacker News (spot check) |
| Unknown Blogs | Low | Personal blogs, unknown domains |
| AI-Generated Content | Low-Medium | ChatGPT, Claude outputs (not primary sources) |

## Red Flags

Watch for these indicators of unreliable sources:

1. **Emotional language** — Uses "shocking," "mind-blowing," "experts are calling this the end of"
2. **No citation** — Makes factual claims without sources
3. **Clickbait title** — Overly sensational, promising definitive answers
4. **No date** — Cannot determine recency
5. **Domain mismatch** — A tech blog claiming health statistics
6. **Single source chain** — Every "source" traces back to one original
7. **Confidential/invisible sources** — "Sources close to..." with no identification
8. **Cherry-picked statistics** — Selective data presentation without context
9. **Conflict of interest** — Company-funded research, sponsored content
10. **AI-generated disclaimers** — No disclosure that content was AI-generated

## Green Flags

Strong sources typically have:

1. **Named, credentialed authors** with clear affiliations
2. **Transparent methodology** — How data was collected and analyzed
3. **Original data or first-hand reporting** — Not repeating secondary sources
4. **Corroborating evidence** — Multiple independent sources agree
5. **Appropriate caveats** — Acknowledges limitations and uncertainty
6. **Clear date** — Published and last updated dates visible
7. **Reputable publication** — Established editorials and fact-checking processes
8. **Balanced tone** — Presents competing viewpoints fairly
9. **Full-text availability** — Content is accessible, not just a snippet
10. **Downloadable data** — Raw data or datasets available for verification

## Quick Assessment Checklist

For each source, answer:

- [ ] Who authored this? Are they credible on this topic?
- [ ] When was this published? Is it current enough?
- [ ] What is the publication venue? Does it have editorial standards?
- [ ] Is the source self-contained or does it cite other evidence?
- [ ] Can the claims be verified independently?
- [ ] Is there a conflict of interest?
- [ ] Does the tone match the claim (measured vs. sensational)?

If 3+ boxes fail, treat the source as low-credibility and seek alternatives.

## Special Cases

### Academic Papers
- Check if peer-reviewed or preprint-only
- Look at citation count (Google Scholar)
- Read abstract + conclusion (not just results)
- Check the references section for their evidence chain

### Corporate Data/Reports
- 10-K filings (SEC) are legally vetted — high trust
- Investor presentations — promotional, cross-check with 10-K
- Press releases — company messaging, never primary evidence
- Third-party data citing company — check methodology

### Statistics
- Always trace back to the original study
- Check sample size and methodology
- Look for confidence intervals or error bars
- Beware of correlation vs. causation framing

### Social Media
- Expert tweets/threads can be valuable but verify
- Check profile credentials carefully
- Look for links to primary sources in replies
- Note: social media is for discovery, not citation
